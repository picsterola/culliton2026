About this guide

A civic resource focused on one constitutional question that quietly shapes tax policy in Washington State. Independent, nonpartisan, noncommercial.

Why this exists

Washington Supreme Court races are among the lowest-information elections on the ballot. Most voters see them at the bottom of the ballot and choose based on incumbency, endorsements, or a coin flip. The result is that the constitutional doctrine governing the state's revenue system can swing on a few percentage points among voters who have no idea the underlying question is even on the ballot.

This guide does not try to fix that with another generic candidate biography. It narrows the field to one durable, high-leverage question — the future of Culliton v. Chase — and shows what the public record says about each candidate on that one question. Voters who care about other dimensions are encouraged to consult the resources linked below.

What this guide is not

  • Not a voter guide on judicial qualifications generally. The Washington State Bar Association and county bar associations do this well.
  • Not a partisan publication. We do not endorse candidates and we are not affiliated with any party, campaign, PAC, or independent expenditure committee.
  • Not a prediction service. The methodology page explains why we do not assign vote-prediction scores.
  • Not a substitute for reading the candidates' own websites and statements.

Other resources voters should consult

  • League of Women Voters of Washington — nonpartisan voter guides covering all races.
  • Washington State Bar Association — judicial candidate ratings.
  • King County Bar Association — county-level judicial ratings.
  • Washington Courts — official biographies and opinions.
  • Public Disclosure Commission — campaign finance filings.

Editorial standards

We aim to describe rather than judge. Where a signal cuts in a particular direction, we say so plainly and explain why. Where the record is ambiguous, we mark it ambiguous rather than guessing. Where a candidate has no record on the underlying question, we say that too. We try not to privilege one school of constitutional methodology over another; we describe what each candidate has actually said and done.

We do not publish anonymous tips, opposition research, or independent expenditure attack material. Every claim on every page should be traceable to a primary public source listed at the bottom of that page.

Corrections and responses

Candidates, campaigns, and members of the public are invited to submit corrections, clarifications, or additional context via the response form. Documented corrections are appended to the relevant page with attribution.

Disclaimers

Photographs of candidates are taken from official government bios, campaign websites, and bar/firm directory pages. They are used under fair-use principles for noncommercial civic education. Any candidate or rights holder who objects to use of a specific image may request its removal via the response form and we will substitute or remove it.

Nothing on this site is legal advice. Constitutional questions are simplified for a general audience; readers seeking authoritative analysis should consult the cited opinions and primary sources directly.
