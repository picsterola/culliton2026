Deep read

How this candidate is likely to rule, and why.

Greg Miller is the fourth and least-defined candidate in the Position 5 field, and the honest assessment is that the public record is too thin to support an income-tax lean even though the circumstantial pattern is mildly keep-coded. He is a Carney Badley Spellman shareholder and a founding member of the firm's Appellate Group, admitted to the Washington bar in 1984 (WSBA No. 14459), with more than a hundred merits appeals briefed and seventy-five-plus argued in the Washington appellate courts and the Ninth Circuit. He clerked at the Washington Court of Appeals Division One, served as a Seattle Municipal Court prosecutor, was staff counsel to the Washington Senate Health and Long-Term Care Committee in 1990, and sits pro tem on the Seattle Municipal Court. His institutional life runs through the King County Bar Association, where he has been a member since 1985, served as a trustee from 2001 to 2004, co-founded the Appellate Law Section in 2005 and chaired it in 2006–2007, has chaired and served on the Judicial Evaluation Committee, and has sat on the Martin Luther King Jr. Luncheon Committee for over twenty years. He is also a founding member of the Washington Appellate Lawyers Association. In 2025 he served as a Special Assistant Attorney General for UW Medicine in a Washington Supreme Court amicus filing, showing comfort representing state institutions. At the same time, in Washington State Hospital Association v. Department of Health, 183 Wn.2d 590 (2015), he successfully argued that the Department of Health exceeded its certificate-of-need authority. He has briefed at least one Washington constitutional question of consequence (WA Water Jet Workers v. Yarbrough, art. II § 29). As of mid-May 2026, Wikipedia lists no endorsements for him, no campaign infrastructure has surfaced, and he has made no located public statement on Article VII, Culliton, the capital gains tax, or ESSB 6346. The coalition signal points away from the conservative overturn camp (no WSRP, no Future 42, no Federalist Society trail; long KCBA and MLK Committee tenure), but coalition is not doctrine, and without his own statement the case for moving him off 'unclear' is not strong enough.

  • Forty-plus years inside the Seattle appellate bar

    Miller has been a Washington lawyer since 1984. He clerked at the Court of Appeals Division I, externed in U.S. District Court, and has practiced appeals continuously for over four decades. He co-founded the King County Bar Association's Appellate Law Section in 2005 and chaired it. He is a founding member of the Washington Appellate Lawyers Association (2000), chaired its judicial CJE programs in 2003 and 2006, and has chaired or served on the KCBA Judicial Evaluation Committee across multiple terms. That is the resume of a career institutionalist embedded in the Seattle legal establishment, not a movement-aligned candidate.

  • Has worked both sides of the state

    In 2025 Miller was retained as a Special Assistant Attorney General representing UW Medicine in a Washington Supreme Court amicus filing (Snyder v. Virginia Mason). In 2015 he argued and won Washington State Hospital Association v. Department of Health, 183 Wn.2d 590, where the court held DOH exceeded its certificate-of-need authority. The pattern is a lawyer who will work for state institutions when retained and challenge them when the doctrine says so. That cuts against any clean ideological read in either direction.

  • Constitutional questions are in his portfolio, just not Article VII

    Miller has briefed at least one notable Washington constitutional case: WA Water Jet Workers Association v. Yarbrough, 151 Wn.2d 470 (2004), challenging a prisoner labor program under Wash. Const. art. II § 29. He has filed amicus briefs on charter-school constitutionality, medical malpractice doctrine, parenting plans, and municipal antitrust. Constitutional argument is in his toolkit. Article VII and tax uniformity are not in the located record, which is the issue.

  • No conservative coalition footprint, no progressive coalition footprint either

    Angelis carries the Democratic establishment, sitting justices, and the governor. Larson carries the Washington State Republican Party, Future 42, and Federalist Society-adjacent training. Amamilo has Justice Whitener and Thurston County. Miller has long-running KCBA leadership and MLK Luncheon Committee tenure, which code mildly center-left in Seattle terms, but no party endorsement, no movement training, no surfaced fundraising. Hugh Spitzer's April 2026 comment that 'there are few conservative candidates running' implicitly excludes him from the overturn coalition. That is suggestive, not dispositive.

The legal context

The precedents this seat will rule on.

  • Culliton v. Chase (1933) — the keystone ruling. Income is property; a graduated income tax violates the uniformity clause.
  • Jensen v. Henneford (1936) — the precedent doing the most direct work against ESSB 6346.
  • Quinn v. State (2023) — capital gains upheld 7–2. The most recent test of the wall.
  • ESSB 6346 (2026) — the 9.9% tax on income over $1M now pending before the court.
