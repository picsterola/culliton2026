# The 2026 Money Map

**A fundraising briefing for donors committed to preserving *Culliton v. Chase* and defeating a Washington state income tax**

*Prepared May 19, 2026. All figures from Washington Public Disclosure Commission filings.*

---

## The bottom line, before the spin

Candidates likely to **scrap** *Culliton* and clear the way for an income tax have raised **$820,000**.

Candidates likely to **keep** *Culliton* and defeat an income tax have raised **$189,000**.

That is a **4.3-to-1 money disadvantage** for our side, six months out from the general election. And here is the part that gets glossed over in most donor pitches: **the math of the court doesn't allow for a few smart concentrated bets to carry the day.** This is a sweep election, or close to it. Spreading dollars thin across five races is a strategy. Concentrating on one or two is a slow-motion loss.

Read the next section before deciding what to fund.

---

## The court math, off-ballot included

Five of the nine seats are on the ballot. **Four are not.** The off-ballot four are locked in until at least 2029, and their lean on *Culliton* is already known.

| Position | Justice | On ballot 2026? | Lean |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | Salvador Mungia | No | Scrap |
| 6 | G. Helen Whitener | No | Scrap |
| 8 | Steven González | No | Scrap |
| 9 | Sheryl Gordon McCloud | No | **Keep** |

**Off-ballot baseline: 3 scrap, 1 keep.**

A *Culliton* majority requires **5 votes out of 9**. Our side starts with McCloud. We need **4 more keeps** from the 5 ballot seats.

That means we can lose **at most one** of the five ballot races and still hold the court. Lose two, and the 93-year wall against an income tax falls on the next ESSB 6346 ruling. There is no "concentrate on the one winnable seat" version of this where we win the court. The math doesn't permit it.

This is uncomfortable, because the fundraising picture isn't a sweep picture. It's a triage picture. Reconciling those two realities is what this document is about.

---

## Why this election matters more than any in a generation

Washington's wall against an income tax is a single 1933 ruling: *Culliton v. Chase*. The legislature passed ESSB 6346 in 2025 to force the court's hand. A single ruling can flip 93 years of precedent. Three of these seats are six-year terms that won't be on the ballot again until 2032.

We do not get this chance again.

---

## What "must sweep" means for donor strategy

If we have to win 4 of 5 races, then the cheapest seat to lose is also the most expensive seat to lose. Every race has to be fundable enough to be live. Spending $200K on Larson and $0 on Stevens is not a winning portfolio if Position 3 is the seat that costs us the majority.

The recommendations below are calibrated to keep all five races **live**, with concentration where the marginal dollar moves a race the most.

---

## The seat-by-seat map

### Position 1 — **Fund Edwards seriously. This race cannot be ceded.**

Colleen Melody (scrap) has $107K cash on hand. Scott Edwards (keep) has $10K. Laura Colberg has not filed.

Edwards is **15:1 outraised** by Melody. Under sweep math, a non-competitive Position 1 means the keep coalition is presumptively down a seat before primary day, and we can only afford to lose one.

**Donor recommendation: $200–400K of combined committee and independent expenditure, deployed by July to make Edwards competitive in the primary.** Issue messaging from a 501(c)(4) on Melody's coalition ties to ESSB 6346 sponsors is the lowest-friction way to spend at scale. Edwards' own committee cannot absorb that much under PDC limits; this requires an outside vehicle.

---

### Position 3 — **Stevens needs real money, not a token.**

Two scrap candidates (Diaz $108K COH, Hawk $97K COH) vs. David Stevens (keep, $10K COH). A $25–50K floor here is functionally a write-off, and under sweep math, write-offs aren't allowed.

The argument for funding this seat seriously: **two scrap candidates likely split the scrap primary vote.** If Diaz and Hawk split roughly evenly, both could land below a unified Stevens in the top-two primary. That's a thin reed, but it's not zero. Without serious funding, Stevens never gets above the noise floor to capitalize on a Diaz-Hawk split.

**Donor recommendation: $150–250K to Stevens or an aligned IE vehicle.** Yes, this is throwing money at a long shot. Under sweep math, every keep candidate has to be a credible long shot, because the alternative is conceding the race and conceding the court. If Stevens cannot clear $75K raised by July 21, reassess.

---

### Position 4 (open seat) — **Birk vs. O'Donnell. Diligence is now urgent, not optional.**

Ian Birk (scrap, $230K COH) leads Sean O'Donnell (lean unclear, $142K COH) by only 1.6 to 1 on cash on hand. The single biggest unknown in this whole memo is **where O'Donnell actually lands on *Culliton***.

Under sweep math, you cannot afford to be agnostic about an unclear candidate in a competitive open-seat race. If O'Donnell is a keep, this seat is a four-way fight worth $200K+ of investment. If he is a scrap or genuinely undeclared, this is a write-off and we need to find a keep alternative (or accept this is the one race we lose).

**Donor recommendation: spend $10–25K on opposition research on O'Donnell's bench record within the next 30 days.** Specifically: tax-related rulings, due process challenges to revenue statutes, public statements, and any signal on Article VII property-tax doctrine. This is the cheapest dollar in the entire memo and the one that resolves the most uncertainty. Until that's done, hold IE powder dry on Position 4 but do not redirect away from it.

---

### Position 5 — **Larson is still the highest-leverage single race. Max it out.**

| Candidate | Lean | Cash on Hand |
|---|---|---:|
| Theo Angelis (incumbent) | Scrap | $124,567 |
| **David Larson** | **Keep** | **$108,368** |
| Sharonda Amamilo | Unclear | $14,221 |
| Greg Miller | Unclear | $0 |

Larson is within $16,000 of an incumbent. High lean confidence. The two unclear candidates are likely cleared in the primary, leaving a head-to-head in November.

This is the seat where the keep side's existing money is already competitive on its own merits. The marginal dollar moves the outcome more here than anywhere else.

**Donor recommendation: maximum legal individual contributions to Larson's committee, bundled aggressively. Then a parallel IE vehicle in the $300–500K range targeting Angelis' coalition ties.** Position 5 is the race where you can outspend the opposition. Do it.

*Counsel note: Washington statewide judicial contribution limits are set by the PDC and adjusted biennially. Confirm the current per-donor cap before structuring bundles at [pdc.wa.gov](https://www.pdc.wa.gov).*

---

### Position 7 — **Don't assume Stephens. Bloom is the stronger anti-income-tax bet.**

Four candidates declared: Debra Stephens (incumbent chief justice, raised $26,500), Todd Bloom, Karim Merchant, David Shelvey. None of the challengers have filed PDC reports yet.

The instinct is to treat Stephens as the banker keep vote. That instinct deserves a second look.

**The case for Bloom over Stephens on the merits:**

- Bloom is a **tax attorney, CPA, CFA, and CFP** ([Cascade PBS, 2024](https://www.cascadepbs.org/politics/2024/07/four-candidates-are-vying-fill-open-wa-supreme-court-seat/)). His entire professional life has been spent on the technical doctrine that decides ESSB 6346. He is the only candidate in the field whose career trains him to read an Article VII property-tax challenge from a taxpayer's perspective.
- He has run as a **Republican for U.S. House in 2016 and 2022** ([Wikipedia, 2026 election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Washington_Supreme_Court_election)). That is the cleanest political signal on the keep side: he is not a partisan keep-by-coincidence, he is a movement-aligned keep.
- Stephens is a sitting chief justice who signed several recent unanimous orders alongside the court's progressive bloc. Her keep label is based on her Quinn dissent posture and her Court of Appeals record on tax cases, not on a track record of fighting income-tax expansion the way Bloom's career has.

**The case for Stephens over Bloom on viability:**

- Incumbency. Washington Supreme Court incumbents virtually never lose, even when poorly funded.
- Bloom lost the 2024 Position 2 primary with **16.9% of the vote** against Mungia and Larson ([Ballotpedia](https://ballotpedia.org/Washington_Supreme_Court_elections,_2024)), raising only **$2,539** that entire cycle. He is a long-shot candidate by every conventional measure.
- Stephens has statewide name recognition. Bloom has none.

**The honest read:** Stephens is more likely to win Position 7. Bloom is more likely to vote our way if he wins it. Under sweep math, both matter — and the worst outcome is treating either as a write-off.

**Donor recommendation: a split position.** Fund Bloom seriously enough to be a real candidate ($75–150K of IE-style messaging on his tax-law credentials) so that if he advances out of the primary, he has a campaign infrastructure. Separately, hold $100–200K in IE reserve for Stephens to release the moment a credible scrap challenger files or third-party scrap money lands against her. This is the seat where two keep candidates can be a feature, not a bug: if Stephens and Bloom both advance to the general, we have two anti-income-tax votes on the November ballot and the scrap coalition has none.

*Caveat: WA's top-two primary means Bloom and Stephens could split the keep vote and let a scrap candidate consolidate the primary. With Stephens as a sitting justice, that risk is low but not zero. Worth monitoring after July 21 filings.*

## Where this leaves us

Three sentences:

1. **The court math requires 4 of 5 ballot wins.** Off-ballot, we start with one keep (McCloud) against three scraps. Lose more than one ballot race, and *Culliton* falls. That includes Position 7 — Stephens is not automatic, and Bloom is the more committed anti-income-tax vote even if he's the less likely to win.

2. **The fundraising disadvantage is real and the strategy has to absorb it.** A 4.3-to-1 money gap means we cannot win on parity at every seat. We can win on focused IE pressure, opposition research, and bundled max-contribution discipline — but not by writing off any race.

3. **The unclear candidates are now the highest-priority research targets.** O'Donnell at Position 4 is the biggest single source of uncertainty in this memo. Resolving his lean is a $25K decision that determines a 6-figure allocation question.

This is one configuration. The principle that matters more than the numbers: **every line item is non-zero.** Zero on any single race is the strategic equivalent of conceding the court.

## The independent-expenditure picture (what these numbers don't show)

Everything above is candidate-committee fundraising only. Independent expenditures by PACs, 501(c)(4)s, and trade associations are not in these totals and can dwarf candidate fundraising in the final ninety days. BIAW, Realtors PAC, and similar have historically spent in WA Supreme Court races at 2–5x candidate committee levels. WEA-PAC, AFSCME, and aligned groups will match on the scrap side.

The leverage of outside money is highest where the seat-level race is tightest. Position 5 is the obvious target. Position 1 is the second-best use because Edwards' own committee cannot scale fast enough on its own.
