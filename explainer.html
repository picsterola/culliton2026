The explainer

Why a 1933 court ruling decides whether you'll ever see a state income tax

No jargon. Plain English. About a five-minute read.

The short version

Washington's constitution, written in 1889, says all property in the state has to be taxed at a uniform rate. In 1933, in a case called Culliton v. Chase, the Washington Supreme Court ruled that income counts as property. That single sentence made a graduated income tax — where higher earners pay a higher rate — essentially unconstitutional in Washington.

That's why Washington funds its government mostly through sales tax, property tax, and various business taxes instead. It's why our sales tax rate keeps creeping up. It's why we have one of the most regressive tax structures in the country. And it's all because of one ruling from the Great Depression.

Why does this come up in 2026?

Five of the nine seats on the Washington Supreme Court are on the ballot this year. The court that decides whether the 1933 rule survives, gets worked around, or finally gets thrown out is about to look very different.

In 2023, the court came close to confronting this. The legislature had passed a capital gains tax. Opponents argued it was an income tax dressed up as something else, which would have violated Culliton. The court, in Quinn v. State, upheld the tax — but only by deciding it was technically an excise tax, not a property tax. The court declined to revisit the 1933 ruling.

That was a workaround, not a fix. Sooner or later, someone will pass a tax that's clearly on income, and the court will have to decide: keep the 1933 rule, or finally retire it.

Why is the politics on this so confusing?

On most issues, you can guess where a judge will land by looking at who endorsed them. On this one, the usual map is flipped.

Likely to keep the rule

People who oppose a state income tax. That includes most Republican-aligned candidates, anti-tax groups, and self-described textualist judges who'd read the constitution strictly the way the 1933 court did. The 1933 ruling is the wall protecting their preferred policy outcome.

Likely to scrap the rule

People who think Washington's tax system is broken and want a progressive income tax. That tends to mean candidates appointed by Democratic governors, civil rights lawyers, and judges with a record of revisiting bad precedent. Getting rid of the 1933 rule is the path to the tax structure they want.

So a candidate who is conservative on criminal justice or property rights might still favor preserving Culliton. And a candidate who's liberal on civil rights might be the one pushing to overturn it. The labels don't carry over neatly.

What this guide isn't trying to do

  • It's not predicting how any candidate will vote. Judges can't pledge votes on specific cases, and we won't pretend otherwise.
  • It's not telling you who to vote for.
  • It's not a complete picture of any candidate. There are lots of things that matter in a Supreme Court justice. This guide focuses on one of them.

What it is doing

For each candidate, we look at four things:

  1. Who put them on their current bench. For sitting justices, this matters a lot — appointing governors tend to share priorities with their appointees. For elected county judges, it matters less.
  2. What they did before. A career civil rights lawyer reads the constitution differently than a career tax lawyer. Background isn't destiny, but it's a signal.
  3. How they describe their own approach. When candidates say they're textualists, or that they admire a particular justice, or that they believe in judicial restraint — those are clues, not promises.
  4. What they've actually said or written about taxes. Direct opinions or public statements on Article VII and Culliton. Often there's nothing, and that absence itself tells you the confidence level we have.

Then we sum it up in one line: likely to keep the rule, likely to scrap it, or not enough to tell. And we show our work so you can disagree.

One more thing
