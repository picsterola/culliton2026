In 1889, when Washington became a state, the constitution required that all property be taxed at a uniform rate. In 1933, the state Supreme Court ruled — by a single vote — that income counts as property. That one sentence is the reason Washington has no graduated income tax. It is also the reason the state has one of the most regressive tax structures in the country.

Every income tax the legislature has tried to pass since has been struck down, rebranded, or designed to dodge that ruling. Culliton v. Chase is the keystone in a chain of decisions stretching from 1908 to 2023 that built the wall. In 2026, the legislature is trying to climb over it again. The court will decide whether the wall holds.

Five of nine seats are on the ballot. This guide does one thing: it reads the public record on each candidate and asks where they're likely to land when the question reaches them.

The chain · 1908 — 2026

How one sentence became a wall.

Culliton isn't a standalone ruling. It's the keystone in a 93-year chain of decisions, each one building on the last. Read left to right: every link is a brick in the wall that blocks a state income tax in Washington.

  1. 1908

    Wolfe v. Parmenter

    Cash is property the instant you receive it. A footnote at the time. The seed for everything that followed.

  2. June 1930

    Aberdeen Sav. & Loan v. Chase

    A tax on net income is a property tax, no matter what the legislature calls it. Substance over label.

  3. Nov 1930

    14th Amendment

    Voters approve a constitutional rewrite. Property now means "everything, whether tangible or intangible, subject to ownership."

  4. Sept 1933

    Culliton v. Chase

    5–4. Income is property. A graduated income tax violates the uniformity clause. The wall.

    KEYSTONE
  5. Sept 1933

    State ex rel. Stiner v. Yelle

    Same day. Excise taxes on specific activities are fine, even if measured by income. The only door left open.

  6. 1936

    Jensen v. Henneford

    You can't rename an income tax "an excise on the privilege of receiving income." Back door closed.

  7. 2023

    Quinn v. State

    Capital gains tax upheld as an excise on the sale of assets. Court declined to revisit Culliton. The workaround held.

  8. 2026

    ESSB 6346 — pending

    9.9% tax on income over $1M, labeled an excise on "the receipt of income." Synonym for the 1936 statute Jensen killed. Litigation pending.

"It would certainly defy the ingenuity of the most profound lexicographer to formulate a more comprehensive definition of property. It is everything, whether tangible or intangible, subject to ownership. Income is either property under our fourteenth amendment, or no one owns it."

— Justice Oscar Holcomb, Culliton v. Chase, September 8, 1933

One last thing

Judicial races decide more than judicial races.

Most voters skip them or pick on incumbency. The result: the rules that decide how a state collects revenue swing on tiny turnout, decided by voters who didn't know the question was on the table.

This guide narrows the field to one durable question. There are others worth caring about — temperament, criminal procedure, civil rights, environmental law. For those, check the League of Women Voters and the Washington State Bar.
