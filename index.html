The 60-second version

A 1933 ruling decided income was "property." That single sentence has shaped Washington's tax code for nine decades.

The Washington Constitution says property has to be taxed at a uniform rate. In 1933, the state Supreme Court said income counted as property. That's why Washington can't have a graduated income tax — and why your sales tax keeps going up instead.

In 2023, the court upheld the capital gains tax by calling it an excise instead of a property tax. The court could have overturned the 1933 ruling. It chose not to.

Whoever sits on the Washington Supreme Court for the next eight years will decide whether that workaround holds, expands, or finally breaks.

Judicial elections are the lowest-information races on your ballot.

Most voters skip them or pick based on incumbency. The result: the rules that decide how your state collects revenue swing on small turnout among voters who didn't know the question was even on the table.

This guide narrows the field to one durable question. There are others worth caring about — judicial temperament, criminal procedure, civil rights, environmental law. For those, please also check the League of Women Voters and the Washington State Bar ratings.
