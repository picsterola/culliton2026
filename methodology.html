Methodology

What this guide does, what it deliberately does not do, and why a single question is more useful than a scorecard for voting in judicial elections.

The question this guide asks

Every candidate page reads against the same underlying question: how might this candidate approach Culliton v. Chase, 174 Wash. 363 (1933), if the question of overruling it reached the court during their term?

Culliton held that income is "property" within the meaning of Article VII §1 of the Washington Constitution, which requires uniform taxation of property. The practical effect: Washington cannot enact a graduated income tax without either overruling Culliton or amending the constitution. The 2023 decision in Quinn v. State upheld the capital gains tax by characterizing it as an excise tax, and the lead opinion expressly declined to revisit Culliton.

This is one of the highest-leverage questions in Washington constitutional law, and it almost never appears in candidate questionnaires.

Why a signals guide instead of a scorecard

Washington's Code of Judicial Conduct (Rule 4.1) prohibits judicial candidates from making pledges or promises about how they will rule on specific cases or issues likely to come before the court. A scorecard that claimed to predict votes would either misrepresent the candidates or invite recusal motions later. Neither serves voters.

What is permissible — and what this guide does — is to surface the public-record signals that voters can read for themselves: who appointed the candidate, what their prior practice looked like, what methodology they have publicly endorsed, what endorsements they have received, and what they have written or said in public forums.

Categories of signal

Each candidate page organizes signals into four standard categories:

  1. Appointing authority. Who placed the candidate on their current bench, and what was that appointer's record on the underlying tax question? For sitting justices, this carries weight; for elected superior or municipal court judges, it usually does not.
  2. Prior practice and background. Career-long professional context — civil rights, tax controversy, prosecution, civil litigation, appellate work — tells you something about how the candidate reads statutes and approaches doctrinal questions.
  3. Methodology and stated influences. When candidates publicly describe their approach to constitutional interpretation, name judicial heroes, or label themselves textualist or pragmatist, those are signals. They are not pledges and they are not perfectly predictive.
  4. Tax / Article VII / takings record. Direct judicial opinions, academic writing, or public commentary on Article VII §1, Culliton, or related doctrine. For candidates without an appellate record, this section is often empty — and the absence itself is a signal about confidence level.

A counterintuitive point about political valence

On most issues, you can predict a Washington judicial candidate's posture by reading their partisan endorsement pattern. On Culliton, the conventional map is inverted.

Candidates aligned with progressive coalitions tend to have the strongest professional incentive to overrule Culliton, because Culliton blocks the graduated income tax that has been a recurring goal of left-of-center tax reformers for nine decades.

Candidates aligned with anti-income-tax coalitions — including some self-described textualists — often have a strong professional incentive to preserve Culliton, because it is the constitutional shield against the policy outcome they oppose. A strict textualist reading of Article VII §1 — "all property shall be taxed uniformly" — entrenches the 1933 court's reading that "property" includes income.

This means a candidate's score on a generic liberal-conservative axis is a poor predictor of how they will approach Culliton. Read the signals on each page, not the partisan shorthand.

What this guide does not claim

  • It does not predict how any candidate will vote on any case.
  • It does not recommend voting for or against any candidate.
  • It does not assign a numerical score to any candidate. Earlier internal drafts did; the published version does not, because numerical scores invite mis-reading as predictions.
  • It does not represent itself as a complete account of any candidate's qualifications. For ratings on judicial temperament, legal ability, and other criteria, consult the Washington State Bar Association and county bar associations.

Sources and corrections

Each candidate page lists the primary public sources used. Where a candidate or campaign believes a signal is mischaracterized or where additional public-record information would change the picture, please use the response form to submit a correction. We will append documented corrections to the relevant page and credit the submission.

Research was conducted using public sources only — court opinions, governor's office appointment releases, candidate campaign sites and questionnaires, Public Disclosure Commission filings, news coverage, and bar association materials. We did not interview candidates, campaign staff, or independent expenditure organizers in preparing the initial version.
